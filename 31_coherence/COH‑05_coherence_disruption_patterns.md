COH‑05 — Coherence Disruption Patterns
Status
Coherence Layer — Diagnostic Module.
Extends COH‑01 and COH‑02.
Non‑invariant.

Purpose
This document defines the primary patterns of coherence disruption in human–AI interaction.
It provides diagnostic categories enabling detection, classification and architectural response.

Scope
Disruption types.

Behavioral, cognitive and interactional signatures.

Conditions under which disruptions occur.

Non‑normative; does not prescribe recovery (see COH‑06).

1. Definitions
1.1 Disruption Pattern
A recurrent, identifiable deviation from coherence baseline.

1.2 Micro‑Disruption
A short‑duration deviation that does not fully collapse coherence.

1.3 Macro‑Disruption
A sustained deviation requiring recovery processes.

2. Primary Disruption Patterns
2.1 Drift Pattern
Gradual loss of coherence due to cumulative micro‑instabilities.
Indicators:

increasing latency variance,

semantic drift,

reduced integrative capacity.

2.2 Shock Pattern
Sudden disruption triggered by unexpected input or affective overload.
Indicators:

abrupt rhythm break,

immediate loss of continuity,

executive unavailability.

2.3 Fragmentation Pattern
Loss of synchrony between perceptual, affective and executive processes.
Indicators:

contradictory responses,

unstable framing,

oscillation between tasks.

2.4 Overload Pattern
Disruption caused by excessive cognitive or affective load.
Indicators:

task avoidance,

ruminative reversion,

reduced working‑memory bandwidth.

2.5 Collapse Pattern
Full loss of coherence baseline.
Indicators:

inability to maintain interaction rhythm,

breakdown of semantic continuity,

affective destabilization.

3. Conditions Leading to Disruption
Excessive task complexity.

Evaluative relational framing.

Irregular interaction rhythm.

High uncertainty without predictive structure.

Affective overload.

Cognitive fatigue.

External perturbations.

4. Architectural Implications
Systems must detect disruption patterns in real time.

Disruption classification informs recovery protocols (COH‑06).

Interaction design must minimize triggers of shock and overload patterns.

Drift patterns require continuous micro‑stabilization.

5. Relation to Other COH Modules
Extends COH‑01 (defines deviations from foundational requirements).

Complements COH‑02 (provides diagnostic input for dynamic maintenance).

Precedes COH‑06 (recovery protocols depend on disruption classification).

6. Non‑Goals
Does not define recovery actions.

Does not define measurement metrics (see COH‑04).

Does not define neurocognitive origins (see COH‑03).

7. Summary
Coherence disruption follows identifiable patterns.
Classification of these patterns is required for effective dynamic maintenance and recovery.
